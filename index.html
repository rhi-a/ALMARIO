THE START OF IT ALL

Often mistaken with the study of dinosaur bones (that's down the left, the Paleontology office), Archaeology, as a field, is the study of the human timeline by examining materials left behind from bygone times. In our work, we study all types of remain left behind by the human species. These are called 'Artifacts'. An artifact can be anything created, destroyed, modified, and used by a human being. The word "Archaeology" is derived from the greek word for ancient, "arkhaios".
The earliest form of technically archaeology is through acts of looting such as grave robbing and ransacking.
It later evolved into a much more formal form after the rise of antiqurianism and the Italian fetish for ancient Greece.
Today, archaeologists study dig sites all over the world to try piece the 15,000 year old puzzle of the entire human story. The history of this study of history is quite a long one, funnily enough. Buy the full issue and read all about it!

TO UNDERSTAND IT ALL

Archaeologists employ many methods and strategies to try and put together an ancient human society.
As mentioned, there are the artifacts.
Artifacts could be anything, from a jagged stone knife to your mother. Other hints, such as geographical location, local ecosystems, non-human remains, and etc, are utilized by archaeologists to further their investigations.
This is what sets apart Archaeology from Paleontology. Where as the latter is the study of mere plant and animal remains, the former is the study of actual life-- societies and dynamics, people and their lives, their culture, their art, their diet, and much more.

THE MINDS BEHIND IT ALL

There are many different fields of archaeology, such as: bioarcheology (which specifies in human remains), zooarcheology (which focuses on animals), paleoethnobotany (the study of old plants), and lithics (concerned with stone workings)
It is difficult to place archaeology in a specific group of science.
Whilst it is mostly acknowledged as a branch of anthropology, some consider it to simply be a study of human history, and the most radical of all think of the field as a social science.
Would an archaeologist be considered a historian? Or is he a scientist? Is he a doctor? Read the full version to hear what real, respected archaeologists have to think.
